# Elderly Care First Aid

Older adults experience more falls, take more medications, and have thinner skin and more fragile bones — so the same incident often has bigger consequences. First aid principles are unchanged but assessment thresholds are lower: call [000](tel:000) sooner rather than later.

Follow [DRSABCD](/kb/drsabcd) for any serious incident.

## Falls

Falls are the leading cause of injury death in Australians over 65.

- Do **not** rush to lift the person up. Assess first.
- Check for pain in the hip, pelvis, wrist or head. Suspected hip fracture: keep them still, support the limb, call [000](tel:000).
- Any head strike on anticoagulants (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) needs hospital assessment **even if they seem fine** — bleeding can be delayed.
- After the fall, ask **why** — dizziness, fainting, low blood sugar, stroke or heart rhythm problem may be the underlying cause.

See [Fractures](/kb/fractures), [Head Injury](/kb/head-injury) and [Spinal Injury](/kb/spinal-injury).

## Heart attack and stroke

Symptoms in older adults are often **atypical**: shortness of breath, sudden fatigue, confusion or a fall instead of classic chest pain.

- Use **FAST** for stroke: Face droop, Arm weakness, Speech change, Time to call [000](tel:000). See [Stroke](/kb/stroke).
- Chest discomfort, breathlessness, nausea or sudden sweating — assume [heart attack](/kb/heart-attack). Sit the person down, call [000](tel:000), 300 mg aspirin if not allergic.

## Medication-related emergencies

- **Anticoagulants** make minor bumps bleed heavily and cause hidden internal bleeding. Apply firm pressure for at least 15 minutes; seek medical review for any significant impact.
- **Blood pressure tablets and diuretics** can cause fainting on standing — sit them down with legs raised. See [Fainting](/kb/fainting).
- **Diabetes medications** (insulin, gliclazide) can cause [hypoglycaemia](/kb/diabetes): confusion, sweating, slurred speech. If conscious and able to swallow, give sugary drink or jellybeans.
- **Opioid pain relief** can suppress breathing. If breathing is slow and the person is hard to rouse, call [000](tel:000); naloxone reverses opioids if available.
- Always show paramedics the **medication list** or the Webster pack.

## Choking

Older adults choke more often due to poor dentition, dry mouth, and swallowing problems (dysphagia) from stroke or dementia.

- 5 back blows, then 5 chest thrusts. **Do not** do abdominal thrusts on a frail elderly person if you can avoid it — chest thrusts are safer. See [Choking](/kb/choking).
- After any choking event with aspirated food, watch for fever, cough or breathlessness over the next 48 hours — aspiration pneumonia is common.

## Skin tears and pressure injuries

Thin, fragile skin tears easily.

- Rinse with saline, gently lay the skin flap back into place, cover with a low-adherent silicone dressing. Do **not** use sticky tape on the skin.
- For a bed- or chair-bound person, reposition every 2 hours to prevent pressure injuries on the heels, sacrum and elbows.

## Heat and cold

Older bodies regulate temperature poorly.

- In a heatwave: cool fluids, fan, wet cloths to neck and armpits. See [Heat Illness](/kb/heat-illness).
- In winter: indoor [hypothermia](/kb/hypothermia) is common in poorly heated homes. Warm the room, warm dry layers, warm drinks if conscious.

## Dementia and delirium

A sudden change in behaviour, confusion or agitation in an older person is **delirium until proven otherwise** — often caused by infection (urine, chest), pain, dehydration or a new medication. It needs medical review, not just reassurance.

## When to call 000

- Head strike on a blood thinner.
- Suspected hip fracture, stroke or heart attack.
- Unconscious, drowsy or hard to rouse.
- Sudden confusion that is not normal for them.
- Severe bleeding that does not stop with 15 minutes of pressure.
- Any fall the person cannot get up from.
