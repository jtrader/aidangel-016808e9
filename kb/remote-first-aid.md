# Regional and Remote First Aid

In remote Australia an ambulance can be hours — sometimes a day — away. First aid in these settings is about **buying time** while help is dispatched, and knowing how to call for it.

In any serious emergency, call [000](tel:000). If there is no mobile signal, try **112** from any GSM phone or **106** by text from a TTY. Satellite phones, PLBs (personal locator beacons) and the **Royal Flying Doctor Service (RFDS)** are core remote-medicine tools.

## Before you go

- Carry a registered **PLB** (406 MHz) on multi-day trips. Activation alerts AMSA who task the RFDS or local rescue.
- Take an **enhanced first aid kit**: large trauma dressings, snake-bite bandages, splints, burn gel sheets, oral rehydration sachets, and any personal medications in duplicate.
- Tell someone your route and expected return. Carry a paper map.
- For very remote travel, do a **Remote Area First Aid** course (HLTAID013) — it covers prolonged casualty care.

## Calling for help when the ambulance is hours away

1. Call [000](tel:000) (or 112). Ask for **ambulance**. Stay on the line.
2. Give **GPS coordinates** if possible. Describe terrain, nearest landmark, and access route for a vehicle or helicopter.
3. Mark a landing zone: 30 × 30 m clear flat ground, no overhead wires, smoke or torch to show wind direction.
4. If no signal: activate the PLB, then move someone to high ground or to the road to flag help.

## Prolonged casualty care principles

When professional help is hours away you become the medical team for longer than a normal first aider expects.

- **Reassess** the casualty every 15 minutes — pulse, breathing, conscious state, skin colour, bleeding.
- **Keep notes** on paper: times, observations, treatments given, medications, fluid in and out. Hand these to paramedics.
- **Protect from the environment:** insulate from the ground, shelter from sun, wind and rain. Hypothermia kills casualties even in warm climates.
- **Hydration:** if conscious and not vomiting, sips of water with oral rehydration salts.
- **Reposition** an unconscious casualty every 30 minutes to prevent pressure injuries. Keep them in the [recovery position](/kb/recovery-position).

## Common remote scenarios

- **[Snake bite](/kb/snake-bite):** Pressure-immobilisation bandage entire limb, splint, do not wash, keep still. RFDS will fly antivenom in.
- **[Heat illness](/kb/heat-illness) and [dehydration](/kb/dehydration):** Move to shade, cool with water and fanning, rehydrate slowly.
- **[Hypothermia](/kb/hypothermia):** Even desert nights drop dangerously cold. Insulate, share body heat, warm drinks if conscious.
- **[Fractures](/kb/fractures):** Splint in position found. Long-bone fractures may need traction if evacuation is delayed many hours — only if trained.
- **Severe [bleeding](/kb/bleeding):** Direct pressure, tourniquet on a limb if uncontrolled. A correctly applied tourniquet can stay on for several hours.
- **Cardiac arrest:** [CPR](/kb/cpr) plus [AED](/kb/aed) if available. Outside AED range, CPR alone has very limited success — but is still attempted while help is mobilised.

## Indigenous and outback communities

Many remote communities have a clinic with a nurse and a satellite link to the RFDS. Know its location and after-hours number before you need it. Cultural protocols matter: ask permission before treating Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander casualties where possible, and respect privacy and Sorry Business if a death occurs.
